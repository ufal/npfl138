# Rotary Positional Embeddings

Instead of _absolute_ positional embeddings, **relative** positional embeddings
have been repeatedly proposed in literature. Such relative positional embeddings
are applied to the query-key similarity $вЖТq_m^\TвЖТk_n$ and depend only on the
difference $m-n$, not on $m$ or $n$.

~~~
One such variant are **rotary positional embeddings** (RoPE), which have become
de-facto standard positional embeddings in large language models.

~~~
They do not require trainable parameters, they scale remarkably well to context
windows of 128k+ tokens, and models trained using RoPE can be finetuned on much
larger sequences in just a fraction of original compute.

---
# Improvements in Modern Large Language Models

# Rotary Positional Embeddings

First assume queries and keys are just 2D vector, which we interpret as
complex numbers. The idea of RoPE is to multiply a query $q_m$ by $e^{imќЄ}$
and a key $k_n$ by $e^{inќЄ}$. Then the cosine of the angle of these two vectors
is
$$\operatorname{Re}[q_m k_n^* e^{i(m-n)ќЄ}].$$

~~~
![w=44%,f=right](rope.svgz)

In reality, we do not compute with complex numbers, but use a 2D rotation
matrix:
$$\begin{gathered}
  \begin{pmatrix} \cos{mќЄ}& -\sin{mќЄ} \\ \sin{mќЄ}&\cos{mќЄ} \\ \end{pmatrix} вЖТq_m,\\
  \begin{pmatrix} \cos{nќЄ}& -\sin{nќЄ} \\ \sin{nќЄ}&\cos{nќЄ} \\ \end{pmatrix} вЖТk_n.
\end{gathered}$$

# Rotary Positional Embeddings

To extend the idea to $d$-dimensional embeddings, we consider them
$d/2$-dimensional complex vector and apply the 2D transformation to
each dimension with $ќЄ_i = 1/10\,000^{2i/d}$ separately.

~~~
$$
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \cos{mќЄ_1}& -\sin{mќЄ_1}&0&0&\cdots&0&0 \\
    \sin{mќЄ_1}&\cos{mќЄ_1}&0&0&\cdots&0&0 \\

$$

# Image Recognition with Transformers

As of April 2025, the current second best model on ImageNet is BASIC-L trained with
Lion optimizer. The image encoder in BASIC-L is CoAtNet-7, an architecture

# Multi-armed Bandits

We start by selecting an action $A_1$ (the index of the arm to use), and we
obtain a reward $R_1$. We then repeat the process by selecting an action $A_2$,
obtaining $R_2$, selecting $A_3$, вА¶, with the indices denoting the time step
when the actions and rewards occurred.

~~~
Let $q_*(a)$ be the real **value** of an action $a$:
$$q_*(a) = рЭФЉ[R_t | A_t = a].$$

~~~
Assuming our goal is to maximize the sum of rewards $\sum_i R_i$, the optimal
strategy is to repeatedly perform the action with the largest value $q_*(a)$.

---
# Multi-armed Bandits

However, we do not know the real action values $q_*(a) = рЭФЉ[R_t | A_t = a]$.

~~~
Therefore, we will try to estimate them, denoting $Q_t(a)$ our estimated value
of action $a$ at time $t$ (before taking the trial $t$).

~~~
A natural way to estimate $Q_t(a)$ is to average the observed rewards:
$$Q_t(a) вЙЭ \frac{\textrm{sum of rewards when action }a\textrm{ is taken}}{\textrm{number of times action }a\textrm{ was taken}}.$$

~~~
Utilizing our estimates $Q_t(a)$, we define the **greedy action** $A_t$ as
$$A_t вЙЭ \argmax_a Q_t(a).$$

~~~
When our estimates are accurate enough, the optimal strategy is to repeatedly
perform the greedy action.

# Law of Large Numbers

Let $X_1, X_2, вА¶, X_n$ be independent and identically distributed (iid) random
variables with finite mean $рЭФЉ\big[X_i\big] = ќЉ < вИЮ$, and let
$$XћД_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i.$$

~~~
## Weak Law of Large Numbers

The average $XћД_n$ converges in probability to $ќЉ$:
$$XћД_n \stackrel{p}{\rightarrow} ќЉ \textrm{~~when~~} n вЖТ вИЮ\textrm{,~~i.e.,~~}\lim_{n вЖТ вИЮ} P\big(|XћД_n - ќЉ|<ќµ\big) = 1.$$

~~~
## Strong Law of Large Numbers

The average $XћД_n$ converges to $ќЉ$ almost surely:
$$XћД_n \stackrel{a.s.}{\longrightarrow} ќЉ \textrm{~~when~~} n вЖТ вИЮ\textrm{,~~i.e.,~~}P\Big(\lim_{n вЖТ вИЮ} XћД_n = ќЉ\Big) = 1.$$

---
# Exploitation versus Exploration

Choosing a greedy action is **exploitation** of current estimates. We however also
need to **explore** the space of actions to improve our estimates.

~~~
To make sure our estimates converge to the true values, we need to sample every
action unlimited number of times.

~~~
An _$ќµ$-greedy_ method follows the greedy action with probability $1-ќµ$, and
chooses a uniformly random action with probability $ќµ$.

# Markov Decision Process

![w=47%,h=center](mdp.svgz)

A **Markov decision process** (MDP) is a quadruple $(рЭУҐ, рЭУР, p, ќ≥)$,
where:
- $рЭУҐ$ is a set of states,
~~~
- $рЭУР$ is a set of actions,
~~~
- $p(S_{t+1} = s', R_{t+1} = r | S_t = s, A_t = a)$ is a probability that
  action $a вИИ рЭУР$ will lead from state $s вИИ рЭУҐ$ to $s' вИИ рЭУҐ$, producing a **reward** $r вИИ вДЭ$,
~~~
- $ќ≥ вИИ [0, 1]$ is a **discount factor** (we always use $ќ≥=1$ and finite episodes in this course).

~~~
Let a **return** $G_t$ be $G_t вЙЭ вИС_{k=0}^вИЮ ќ≥^k R_{t + 1 + k}$. The goal is to optimize $рЭФЉ[G_0]$.

---
# Episodic and Continuing Tasks

If the agent-environment interaction naturally breaks into independent
subsequences, usually called **episodes**, we talk about **episodic tasks**.
Each episode then ends in a special **terminal state**, followed by a reset
to a starting state (either always the same, or sampled from a distribution
of starting states).

~~~
In episodic tasks, it is often the case that every episode ends in at most
$H$ steps. These **finite-horizon tasks** then can use discount factor $ќ≥=1$,
because the return $G вЙЭ вИС_{t=0}^H ќ≥^t R_{t + 1}$ is well defined.

~~~
If the agent-environment interaction goes on and on without a limit, we instead
talk about **continuing tasks**. In this case, the discount factor $ќ≥$ needs
to be sharply smaller than 1.


---
# Policy

A **policy** $ѕА$ computes a distribution of actions in a given state, i.e.,
$ѕА(a | s)$ corresponds to a probability of performing an action $a$ in state
$s$.

~~~
We will model a policy using a neural network with parameters $вЖТќЄ$:
$$ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ).$$

~~~
If the number of actions is finite, we consider the policy to be a categorical
distribution and utilize the $\softmax$ output activation as in supervised
classification.

---
# (State-)Value Function and Action-Value Function

To evaluate a quality of a policy, we define **value function** $v_ѕА(s)$, or
**state-value function**, as
$$\begin{aligned}
  v_ѕА(s) & вЙЭ рЭФЉ_ѕА\big[G_t \big| S_t = s\big] = рЭФЉ_ѕА\left[вИС\nolimits_{k=0}^вИЮ ќ≥^k R_{t+k+1} \middle| S_t=s\right] \\
         & = рЭФЉ_{A_t вИЉ ѕА(s)} рЭФЉ_{S_{t+1},R_{t+1} вИЉ p(s,A_t)} \big[R_{t+1} + ќ≥v_ѕА(S_{t+1})\big] \\
         & = рЭФЉ_{A_t вИЉ ѕА(s)} рЭФЉ_{S_{t+1},R_{t+1} вИЉ p(s,A_t)} \big[R_{t+1}
           + ќ≥ рЭФЉ_{A_{t+1} вИЉ ѕА(S_{t+1})} рЭФЉ_{S_{t+2},R_{t+2} вИЉ p(S_{t+1},A_{t+1})} \big[R_{t+2} + вА¶ \big]\big]
\end{aligned}$$

~~~
An **action-value function** for a policy $ѕА$ is defined analogously as
$$q_ѕА(s, a) вЙЭ рЭФЉ_ѕА\big[G_t \big| S_t = s, A_t = a\big] = рЭФЉ_ѕА\left[вИС\nolimits_{k=0}^вИЮ ќ≥^k R_{t+k+1} \middle| S_t=s, A_t = a\right].$$

~~~
The value function and the state-value function can be easily expressed using one another:
$$\begin{aligned}
  v_ѕА(s) &= рЭФЉ_{aвИЉѕА}\big[q_ѕА(s, a)\big], \\
  q_ѕА(s, a) &= рЭФЉ_{s', r вИЉ p}\big[r + ќ≥v_ѕА(s')\big]. \\
\end{aligned}$$

# Optimal Value Functions

**Optimal state-value function** is defined as
$$v_*(s) вЙЭ \max_ѕА v_ѕА(s),$$
~~~
and **optimal action-value function** is defined analogously as
$$q_*(s, a) вЙЭ \max_ѕА q_ѕА(s, a).$$

~~~
Any policy $ѕА_*$ with $v_{ѕА_*} = v_*$ is called an **optimal policy**. Such policy
can be defined as $ѕА_*(s) вЙЭ \argmax_a q_*(s, a) = \argmax_a рЭФЉ\big[R_{t+1} + ќ≥v_*(S_{t+1}) | S_t = s, A_t = a\big]$.
When multiple actions maximize $q_*(s, a)$, the optimal policy can
stochastically choose any of them.

~~~
## Existence
In finite-horizon tasks or if $ќ≥ < 1$, there always exists a unique optimal
state-value function, a¬†unique optimal action-value function, and a (not necessarily
unique) optimal policy for a given MDP with a finite number of states and
a finite number of actions.

# Policy Gradient Methods

We train the policy
$$ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ)$$
by maximizing the expected return $v_ѕА(s)$.

~~~
To that account, we need to compute its **gradient** $вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} v_ѕА(s)$.

---
# Policy Gradient Theorem

Assume that $рЭУҐ$ and $рЭУР$ are finite, $ќ≥=1$, and that maximum episode length $H$ is also finite.

Let $ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ)$ be a parametrized policy. We denote the initial state
distribution as $h(s)$ and the on-policy distribution under $ѕА$ as $ќЉ(s)$.
Let also $J(вЖТќЄ) вЙЭ рЭФЉ_{sвИЉh} v_ѕА(s)$.

~~~
Then
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} v_ѕА(s) вИЭ вИС_{s'вИИрЭУҐ} P(s вЖТ вА¶ вЖТ s'|ѕА) вИС_{a вИИ рЭУР} q_ѕА(s', a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s'; вЖТќЄ)$$
~~~
and
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} J(вЖТќЄ) вИЭ вИС_{sвИИрЭУҐ} ќЉ(s) вИС_{a вИИ рЭУР} q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ),$$

~~~
where $P(s вЖТ вА¶ вЖТ s'|ѕА)$ is the probability of being in state $s'$ when
considering trajectories starting in $s$ and following $ѕА$.

---
# Proof of Policy Gradient Theorem

$\displaystyle вИЗv_ѕА(s) = вИЗ \Big[ вИС\nolimits_a ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) q_ѕА(s, a) \Big]$

~~~
$\displaystyle \phantom{вИЗv_ѕА(s)} = вИС\nolimits_a \Big[ q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) + ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) вИЗ q_ѕА(s, a) \Big]$

~~~
$\displaystyle \phantom{вИЗv_ѕА(s)} = вИС\nolimits_a \Big[ q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) + ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) вИЗ \big(вИС\nolimits_{s', r} p(s', r|s, a)(r + v_ѕА(s'))\big) \Big]$

~~~
$\displaystyle \phantom{вИЗv_ѕА(s)} = вИС\nolimits_a \Big[ q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) + ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) \big(вИС\nolimits_{s'} p(s'|s, a) вИЗ v_ѕА(s')\big) \Big]$

~~~
_We now expand $v_ѕА(s')$._

~~~
$\displaystyle \phantom{вИЗv_ѕА(s)} = вИС\nolimits_a \Big[ q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) + ѕА(a|s; вЖТќЄ) \Big(вИС\nolimits_{s'} p(s'|s, a)\Big(\\
                \quad\qquad\qquad вИС\nolimits_{a'} \Big[ q_ѕА(s', a') вИЗ ѕА(a'|s'; вЖТќЄ) + ѕА(a'|s'; вЖТќЄ) \big(вИС\nolimits_{s''} p(s''|s', a') вИЗ v_ѕА(s'')\big) \Big] \Big) \Big) \Big]$

~~~
_Continuing to expand all $v_ѕА(s'')$, we obtain the following:_

$\displaystyle вИЗv_ѕА(s) = вИС\nolimits_{s'вИИрЭУҐ} вИС\nolimits_{k=0}^H P(s вЖТ s'\textrm{~in~}k\textrm{~steps~}|ѕА) вИС\nolimits_{a вИИ рЭУР} q_ѕА(s', a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s'; вЖТќЄ).$

---
# Proof of Policy Gradient Theorem

The first part of the proof is therefore finished when we use that
$$вИС_{k=0}^вИЮ P(s вЖТ s'\textrm{~in~}k\textrm{~steps~}|ѕА) вИЭ P(s вЖТ вА¶ вЖТ s'|ѕА).$$

~~~
For the second part, we now know that
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} J(вЖТќЄ) = рЭФЉ_{s вИЉ h} вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} v_ѕА(s) вИЭ рЭФЉ_{s вИЉ h} вИС_{s'вИИрЭУҐ} P(s вЖТ вА¶ вЖТ s'|ѕА) вИС_{a вИИ рЭУР} q_ѕА(s', a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s'; вЖТќЄ),$$
~~~
therefore using the fact that $ќЉ(s') = рЭФЉ_{s вИЉ h} P(s вЖТ вА¶ вЖТ s'|ѕА)$ we get
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} J(вЖТќЄ) вИЭ вИС_{sвИИрЭУҐ} ќЉ(s) вИС_{a вИИ рЭУР} q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ).$$

~~~
Finally, note that the theorem can be proven with infinite $рЭУҐ$ and $рЭУР$; and
also for infinite episodes when discount factor $ќ≥<1$.

---
# REINFORCE Algorithm

The REINFORCE algorithm (Williams, 1992) directly uses the policy gradient
theorem, minimizing $-J(вЖТќЄ) вЙЭ -рЭФЉ_{sвИЉh} v_ѕА(s)$. The loss gradient is then
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} -J(вЖТќЄ) вИЭ -вИС_{sвИИрЭУҐ} ќЉ(s) вИС_{a вИИ рЭУР} q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ) = -рЭФЉ_{s вИЉ ќЉ} вИС_{a вИИ рЭУР} q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ).$$

~~~
However, the sum over all actions is problematic. Instead, we rewrite it to an
expectation, which we can estimate by sampling:
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} -J(вЖТќЄ) вИЭ рЭФЉ_{s вИЉ ќЉ} рЭФЉ_{a вИЉ ѕА} q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} -\log ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ),$$
~~~
where we used the fact that
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} \log ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ) = \frac{1}{ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ)} вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ).$$

---
# REINFORCE Algorithm

REINFORCE therefore minimizes the loss $-J(вЖТќЄ)$ with gradient
$$рЭФЉ_{s вИЉ ќЉ} рЭФЉ_{a вИЉ ѕА} q_ѕА(s, a) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} -\log ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ),$$
where we estimate the $q_ѕА(s, a)$ by a single sample.

~~~
Note that the loss is just a weighted variant of negative log-likelihood (NLL),
where the sampled actions play the role of gold labels and are weighted according
to their return.

~~~

# REINFORCE with Baseline

The returns can be arbitrary: better-than-average and worse-than-average
returns cannot be recognized from the absolute value of the return.

~~~
Fortunately, we can generalize the policy gradient theorem using a baseline $b(s)$
to
$$вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} J(вЖТќЄ) вИЭ вИС_{sвИИрЭУҐ} ќЉ(s) вИС_{a вИИ рЭУР} \big(q_ѕА(s, a) - \boldsymbol{b(s)}\big) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ).$$

~~~
The baseline $b(s)$ can be a function or even a random variable, as long as it
does not depend on $a$, because
$$вИС_a b(s) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ) = b(s) вИС_a вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ) = b(s) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} вИС_a ѕА(a | s; вЖТќЄ) = b(s) вИЗ_{вЖТќЄ} 1 = 0.$$

---
# REINFORCE with Baseline

A good choice for $b(s)$ is $v_ѕА(s)$, which can be shown to minimize the
variance of the gradient estimator in some sense (see L. Weaver and N. Tao,
_The Optimal Reward Baseline for Gradient-Based Reinforcement Learning_,
https://arxiv.org/abs/1301.2315, for details).
Such baseline reminds centering of returns, given that
$$v_ѕА(s) = рЭФЉ_{a вИЉ ѕА} q_ѕА(s, a).$$

~~~
Then, better-than-average returns are positive and worse-than-average returns
are negative.

~~~
Of course, we need a way to estimate the $v_ѕА(s)$ baseline. The usual approach
is to approximate it by another neural network model. That model is trained
using mean square error of the predicted and observed returns.

---

In REINFORCE with baseline, we train:
1. the _policy network_ using the REINFORCE algorithm, and
~~~
2. the _value network_ by minimizing the mean squared error.

# EfficientNet Search

EfficientNet changes the search in three ways.

~~~
- Computational requirements are part of the return. Notably, the goal is to
  find an architecture $m$ maximizing
  $$\operatorname{DevelopmentAccuracy}(m) вЛЕ \left(\frac{\textrm{TargetFLOPS=400M}}{\operatorname{FLOPS}(m)}\right)^{0.07},$$
~~~
  where the constant $0.07$ balances the accuracy and FLOPS (_the constant comes
  from an empirical observation that doubling the FLOPS brings about 5% relative
  accuracy gain, and $1.05 = 2^ќ≤$_ gives $ќ≤ вЙИ 0.0704$).

~~~
- It uses a different search space allowing to control kernel sizes and
  channels in different parts of the architecture (compared to using the same
  cell everywhere as in NASNet).

~~~
- Training directly on ImageNet, but only for 5 epochs.

~~~
In total, 8k model architectures are sampled, and PPO algorithm is used
instead of the REINFORCE with baseline.
