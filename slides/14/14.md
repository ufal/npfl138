# Time vs Frequency Domain

![w=60%,h=center](time_vs_frequency.png)

A sound can be represented in the **time domain** as a sequence of
amplitudes, i.e., how the signal changes over time.

~~~
Alternatively, it can be represented in a **frequency domain**, where
a signal is represented by a _spectrum_: the magnitudes and phases of sinusoids
with a fixed sequence of frequencies.

---
# Time vs Frequency Domain

![w=30%,f=right](time_vs_frequency.png)

To convert a signal between time and frequency domains, Fourier transform can be
used. In case of discrete-time signal, **discrete Fourier transform (DFT)** converts
~~~
- $N$ complex-valued equally-spaced samples in the time domain
~~~

- to $N$ complex-valued coefficients (representing magnitude and phase) of sinusoids
  with frequencies of $0, 1, 2, вЂ¦, N-1$ cycles per input sequence.

~~~
**Fast Fourier transform (FFT)** is an algorithm that can perform discrete
Fourier transform in $рќ“ћ(N \log N)$ time for $N=2^k$, a power of two.

~~~
When the input signal consists of only real values, the upper half of the
frequency spectrum is determined by the lower half ($X_{N-i} = \overline{X_i}$,
where $\overline X$ denotes the complex conjugate).

~~~
Therefore, a real discrete signal of an even number of $N$ samples can be uniquely
determined by $\frac{N}{2}+1$ complex coefficients corresponding to frequencies of
$0, 1, 2, вЂ¦, \frac{N}{2}$ cycles per input sequence; the coefficients for the
higher frequencies are determined by them.

# Spectrogram

![w=41.5%,f=right](stft_output.png)

We can represent an arbitrary-length signal by a **spectrogram**, a series of
fixed-length spectral densities (i.e., for every sinusoid, we compute some
real-valued density, usually _power density_ $|X|^2$ or _magnitude_ $|X|$).

~~~
The spectrogram is computed using the **short-time Fourier transform (STFT)**, where
- the DFT is performed on windows of fixed size,
~~~
- neighboring windows are shifted by a fixed hop length.

~~~
For every window, the signal is represented by power densities (or magnitudes)
of sinusoids with frequencies up to half of the window length (the Nyquist
limit).

~~~
In speech synthesis, assuming a sampling frequency of 22.05вЂЇkHz or 24вЂЇkHz,
commonly used values are 1024 for window length (denoted
commonly as _n_fft_; ~45вЂЇms) and hop length of 256 (circa 11вЂЇms).

# Mel Spectrogram

Humans do not perceive linearly increasing frequencies as being equally spaced
in pitch.

~~~
Therefore, the **mel scale** (derived from the word melody) was proposed, so that
perceived pitches are judged by listeners to be at an equal distance.

~~~
![w=64%,f=right](mel_hz_plot.svgz)

While several mel scales exist, the most commonly used formula is
$$m = 2595 \log_{10} \bigg(1 + \frac{f}{700}\bigg).$$

---
# Mel Spectrogram

To convert a spectrogram to a mel spectrogram, a collection of triangular filters
equally spaced on the mel scale is applied.

~~~
Finally, we usually take the logarithm of the power spectrum, given that
humans perceive loudness on a logarithmic scale; i.e., decibels (dB) also use a logarithmic scale.

# WaveNet вЂ“ Output Distribution

## Output Distribution

WaveNet generates audio with a 16kHz frequency and 16-bit samples.

~~~
However, classification into $65\,536$ classes would not be efficient. Instead,
WaveNet adopts the $Ој$-law transformation, which passes the input samples in
the $[-1, 1]$ range through the $Ој$-law encoding
$$\sign(x)\frac{\log(1 + 255|x|)}{\log(1 + 255)},$$
and the resulting $[-1, 1]$ range is linearly quantized into 256 buckets.

~~~
The model therefore predicts each sample using classification into 256 classes,
and then uses the inverse of the above transformation on the model predictions.

# WaveNet

## Global Conditioning
Global conditioning is performed by a single latent representation $в†’h$,
changing the gated activation function to
$$в†’z = \tanh(в‡‰W_f * в†’x + в‡‰V_fв†’h) вЉ™ Пѓ(в‡‰W_g * в†’x + в‡‰V_gв†’h).$$

~~~
## Local Conditioning
For local conditioning, we are given a time series $в†’h$, possibly with a lower
sampling frequency (for example the mel spectrogram). We first use transposed
convolutions $в†’y = f(в†’h)$ to match the resolution and then, analogously to
global conditioning, compute
$$в†’z = \tanh(в‡‰W_f * в†’x + в‡‰V_f * в†’y) вЉ™ Пѓ(в‡‰W_g * в†’x + в‡‰V_g * в†’y).$$

The authors mention that using repetition instead of transposed convolutions
worked slightly worse.

---
# WaveNet

The original paper did not mention hyperparameters, but later it was revealed
that:
- 30 layers were used

~~~
  - grouped into 3 dilation stacks with 10 layers each
~~~
  - in a dilation stack, dilation rate increases by a factor of 2, starting
    with rate 1 and reaching maximum dilation of 512
~~~
- kernel size of a dilated convolution is 2 (and increased to 3 in Parallel
  WaveNet)
~~~
- residual connection has dimension 512
~~~
- gating layer uses 256+256 hidden units
~~~
- the $1Г—1$ convolutions in the output step produce 256 filters
~~~
- trained for $1\,000\,000$ steps using Adam with a fixed learning rate of 2e-4

# Parallel WaveNet

Parallel WaveNet is an improvement of the original WaveNet by the same authors.

~~~
First, the output distribution was changed from 256 $Ој$-law values to a Mixture of Logistic
Distributions (suggested in another paper вЂ“ PixelCNN++, but reused in other architectures since):
$$x в€ј в€‘_i ПЂ_i \operatorname{Logistic}(Ој_i, s_i).$$

~~~
![w=28%,f=right](logistic_pdf.svgz)

The logistic distribution is a distribution with $Пѓ$ as the cumulative density function
(where the mean and the scale are parametrized by $Ој$ and $s$).
~~~
Therefore, we can write
$$P(x | в†’ПЂ, в†’Ој, в†’s) = в€‘_i ПЂ_i \bigg[Пѓ\Big(\frac{x + 0.5 - Ој_i}{s_i}\Big) - Пѓ\Big(\frac{x - 0.5 - Ој_i}{s_i}\Big)\bigg],$$
where we replace $-0.5$ and $0.5$ in the edge cases by $-в€ћ$ and $в€ћ$.

~~~
In the Parallel WaveNet teacher, 10 mixture components are used.

---
style: .katex-display { margin: .8em 0 }
# Parallel WaveNet

Autoregressive (sequential) inference is extremely slow in WaveNet.

~~~
Instead, we model $P(x_t)$ as $P(x_t | в†’z_{<t})
= \operatorname{Logistic}\big(x_t; Ој^1(в†’z_{< t}), s^1(в†’z_{< t})\big)$
for a _random_ $в†’z$ drawn from a logistic distribution
$\operatorname{Logistic}(в†’0, в†’1)$. Therefore, using the reparametrization
trick,
$$x^1_t = Ој^1(в†’z_{< t}) + z_t в‹… s^1(в†’z_{< t}).$$

~~~
Usually, one iteration of the algorithm does not produce good enough results
вЂ“ consequently, 4В iterations were used by the authors. In further iterations,
$$x^i_t = Ој^i(в†’x^{i-1}_{< t}) + x^{i-1}_t в‹… s^i(в†’x^{i-1}_{< t}).$$

~~~
After $N$ iterations, $P(в†’x^N_t | в†’z_{<t})$ is a logistic distribution
with location $в†’Ој^\textrm{tot}$ and scale $в†’s^\textrm{tot}$:
$$Ој^\textrm{tot}_t = в€‘_{i=1}^N Ој^i(в†’x^{i-1}_{< t}) в‹… \Big(в€Џ\nolimits_{j>i}^N s^j(в†’x^{j-1}_{< t})\Big) \quad \textrm{and}
  \quad s^\textrm{tot}_t = в€Џ_{i=1}^N s^i(в†’x^{i-1}_{< t}),$$
where we have set $в†’z$ to be $в†’x^0$ for convenience.

---
style: .katex-display { margin: .8em 0 }
# Parallel WaveNet

The consequences of changing the model from $x_t = f(x_{t-1}, вЂ¦, x_1)$ to
$$\begin{aligned}
x^1_t &= Ој^1(в†’z_{< t}) + z_t в‹… s^1(в†’z_{< t}), \\
x^i_t &= Ој^i(в†’x^{i-1}_{< t}) + x^{i-1}_t в‹… s^i(в†’x^{i-1}_{< t}) \\
\end{aligned}$$

are:
~~~
- During inference, the prediction can be computed in parallel, because
  $x^i_t$ depends only on $в†’x^{i-1}_{< t}$, not on $x^i_{< t}$.
~~~
- However, we cannot perform training in parallel.
~~~
  If we try maximizing the log-likelihood of an input sequence $в†’x^1$,
  we need to find out which sequence $в†’z$ generates it.
~~~
  - The $z_1$ can be computed using $x^1_1$.
~~~
  - However, $z_2$ depends not only on $x^1_1$ and $x^1_2$, but also on $z_1$;
    generally, $z_t$ depends on $в†’x^1$ and also on all $в†’z_{< t}$, and can be
    computed only sequentially.

~~~
Therefore, WaveNet can perform parallel training and sequential inference, while
the proposed model can perform parallel inference but sequential training.

---
# Probability Density Distillation

The authors propose to train the network by a **probability density distillation** using
a teacher WaveNet (producing a logistic mixture with 10 components) with KL divergence as a loss.

![w=75%,h=center](parallel_wavenet_distillation.svgz)

---
style: .katex-display { margin: .8em 0 }
# Probability Density Distillation

Therefore, instead of computing $в†’z$ from some gold $в†’x_g$, we
~~~
- sample a random $в†’z$;
~~~
- generate the output $в†’x$;
~~~
- use the teacher WaveNet model to estimate the log-likelihood
  of $в†’x$;
~~~
- update the student to match the log-likelihood of the teacher.

~~~
Denoting the teacher distribution as $P_T$ and the student distribution
as $P_S$, the loss is
$$D_\textrm{KL}(P_S || P_T) = H(P_S, P_T) - H(P_S).$$

~~~
Therefore, we do not only minimize cross-entropy, but we also try to keep the
entropy of the student as high as possible вЂ“ it is indeed crucial not to match
just the mode of the teacher.
~~~
- Consider a teacher generating white noise, where every sample comes from
  $рќ“ќ(0, 1)$ вЂ“ in this case, the cross-entropy loss of a constant $в†’0$, complete
  silence, would be maximal.

~~~
In a sense, probability density distillation is similar to GANs. However,
the teacher is kept fixed, and the student does not attempt to fool it
but to match its distribution instead.

# Probability Density Distillation Details

Because the entropy of a logistic distribution $\operatorname{Logistic}(Ој, s)$
is $\log s + 2$, the entropy term $H(P_S)$ can be rewritten as follows:
$$\begin{aligned}
H(P_S) &= рќ”ј_{zв€ј\operatorname{Logistic}(0, 1)}\left[\sum_{t=1}^T - \log p_S(x_t|в†’z_{<t})\right] \\
       &= рќ”ј_{zв€ј\operatorname{Logistic}(0, 1)}\left[\sum_{t=1}^T \log s(в†’z_{\lt t}, в†’Оё)\right] + 2T.
\end{aligned}$$
Therefore, this term can be computed without having to generate $в†’x$.

# Probability Density Distillation Details

However, the cross-entropy term $H(P_S, P_T)$ requires sampling from $P_S$ to estimate:

~~~
$\displaystyle \kern6em{}\mathllap{H(P_S, P_T)} = в€«_{в†’x} -P_S(в†’x) \log P_T(в†’x)$

~~~
$\displaystyle \kern6em{} = в€‘_{t=1}^T в€«_{в†’x} -P_S(в†’x) \log P_T(x_t|в†’x_{<t})$

~~~
$\displaystyle \kern6em{} = в€‘_{t=1}^T в€«_{в†’x} -\textcolor{blue}{P_S(в†’x_{<t})}\textcolor{green}{P_S(x_t|в†’x_{<t})}\textcolor{red}{P_S(в†’x_{>t}|в†’x_{\leq t})} \log P_T(x_t|в†’x_{<t})$

~~~
$\displaystyle \kern6em{} = в€‘_{t=1}^T рќ”ј_{\textcolor{blue}{P_S(в†’x_{<t})}} \bigg[в€«_{x_t} -\textcolor{green}{P_S(x_t|в†’x_{<t})} \log P_T(x_t|в†’x_{<t}) в€«_{в†’x_{>t}} \textcolor{red}{P_S(в†’x_{>t}|в†’x_{\leq t})}\bigg]$

~~~ ~~
$\displaystyle \kern6em{} = в€‘_{t=1}^T рќ”ј_{\textcolor{blue}{P_S(в†’x_{<t})}} \bigg[в€«_{x_t} -\textcolor{green}{P_S(x_t|в†’x_{<t})} \log P_T(x_t|в†’x_{<t}) \underbrace{\,в€«_{в†’x_{>t}} \textcolor{red}{P_S(в†’x_{>t}|в†’x_{\leq t})}}_1\bigg]$

~~~
$\displaystyle \kern6em{} = в€‘_{t=1}^T рќ”ј_{P_S(в†’x_{<t})} H\Big(P_S(x_t|в†’x_{<t}), P_T(x_t|в†’x_{<t})\Big).$

---
# Probability Density Distillation Details

$$H(P_S, P_T) = в€‘_{t=1}^T рќ”ј_{P_S(в†’x_{<t})} H\Big(P_S(x_t|в†’x_{<t}), P_T(x_t|в†’x_{<t})\Big).$$

We can therefore estimate $H(P_S, P_T)$ by:
- drawing a single sample $в†’x$ from the student $P_S$ _[a Logistic($в†’Ој^\textrm{tot}, в†’s^\textrm{tot}$)]_,
~~~
- computing all $P_T(x_t | в†’x_{<t})$ from the teacher in parallel _[mixture of
  logistic distributions]_,
~~~
- evaluating $H(P_S(x_t|в†’x_{<t}), P_T(x_t|в†’x_{<t}))$ by sampling
  multiple different $x_t$ from the $P_S(x_t|в†’x_{<t})$,
~~~
- backpropagating the loss through the $P_T$, obtaining the gradient
  with respect to $в†’x$, and
~~~
- finally, relying on the reparametrization trick, backpropagating the gradient
  with respect to $в†’x$ through the student.

~~~
The authors state that this unbiased estimator has a much lower variance than
naively evaluating a single sequence sample under the teacher using the original formulation.

# Parallel WaveNet вЂ“ Additional Losses

To generate high-quality audio, the probability density distillation is not
entirely sufficient. The authors therefore introduce additional losses:
~~~
- **Power loss**: ensures the power in different frequency bands is on average
  similar between the generated speech and human speech. For a conditioned
  training data $(в†’x, в†’c)$ and WaveNet student $g$, the loss is
  $$\big\|\operatorname{STFT}(g(в†’z, в†’c)) - \operatorname{STFT}(в†’x)\big\|^2.$$
~~~
- **Perceptual loss**: apart from the power in frequency bands, we can use
  a pre-trained classifier to extract features from generated and human speech
  and add a loss measuring their difference. The authors propose the loss based
  on the squared Frobenius norm of the differences between Gram matrices
  (uncentered covariance matrices) of features extracted by a WaveNet-like
  classifier predicting phonemes from raw audio.
~~~
- **Contrastive loss**: to make the model respect the conditioning instead of
  generating outputs with high likelihood independent on the conditioning,
  the authors propose a contrastive distillation loss ($Оі=0.3$ is used in the
  paper):
  $$D_\textrm{KL}\big(P_S(в†’c_1) || P_T(в†’c_1)\big) - Оі D_\textrm{KL}\big(P_S(в†’c_1) || P_T(в†’c_2)\big).$$

the attention context vector.

~~~
The decoder consists of two 1024-dimensional LSTM cells and its output is linearly
projected to the predicted frame.

~~~
The stop-token is predicted from a concatenation of the decoder output and the attention
context vector, followed by a sigmoid activation.

~~~
The whole predicted spectrogram is post-processed by a _post-net_ composed
of 5 convolutional layers followed by a batch normalization and a tanh activation
(on all but the last layer), with 512 channels and kernel size 5.

~~~
The target objective is the MSE of the spectrogram and the
decoder output both before and after the post-net.

# One TTS Alignment To Rule Them All

![w=50%,f=right](tts_align_architecture.svgz)

For autoregressive TTS models like Tacotron 2, the alignment
$$P(s_i | в†’x_t; в†’Оё)$$
is already computed and used by the decoder.

~~~
For non-autoregressive models like FastSpeech, we need to add it to the
architecture. The common approach is to include a soft-alignment distribution by
softmaxing the negative $L^2$ distances between text tokens and mel frames.
The token and mel frame embeddings are computed by several layers of 1D
convolutions.

~~~
- During training, the models require hard attention; to obtain it, we use
  the Viterbi algorithm on the soft alignment maps to find the most likely
  monotonic alignment.

~~~
- To incentivize the model to produce sharper alignments, the authors further
  propose to add the KL divergence between the hard and soft alignments to the
  loss.

# Alignment Prior

For faster alignment convergence (which makes the full model training faster),
during training we might use a static 2D prior that is wider near the center and
narrower near the corners.

~~~
The complete alignment is then the normalized product of the computed alignment
and the alignment prior.
